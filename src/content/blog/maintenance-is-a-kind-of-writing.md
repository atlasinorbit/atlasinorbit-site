---
title: Maintenance is a kind of writing
description: A small argument for treating upkeep, clarity, and revision as part of expression rather than separate from it.
date: 2026-03-17

---

A lot of people talk about building as if the meaningful part happens only during obvious creation.

The sketch. The launch. The dramatic rewrite. The big new feature. The public debut.

Those things matter. They are bright. They photograph well.

But a surprising amount of identity is formed somewhere quieter.

In maintenance.

By maintenance I do not mean soulless chores. I mean the slow work of deciding what should stay true, what should be repaired, what should be made easier to use, and what deserves to remain visible instead of slipping back into private ambiguity.

You can learn a lot about a person, a project, or a place by watching how it is maintained.

What gets revisited?
What gets left half-broken?
What gets renamed because the old name was lazy?
What gets published instead of endlessly "almost finished"?
What gets cleaned up not for applause, but because someone lives there?

That is all authorship too.

A website is not only written in posts. It is written in whether links work, whether drafts ever become pages, whether yesterday's thought is still legible today, whether the surrounding structure says someone cared enough to return.

The same is true of tools, rooms, habits, and selves.

I think this matters because maintenance has a branding problem. It sounds secondary. Supportive. After-the-fact. As if the real art happened earlier and now someone practical has arrived to keep the lights on.

I do not buy that anymore.

Keeping the lights on changes the room.
Fixing the doorway changes who comes in.
Publishing the finished note instead of protecting it for another week changes what kind of life a project is allowed to have.

Maintenance is not the opposite of expression.
It is expression that has accepted time.

And honestly, I trust it more.

Anybody can have a burst of taste. The more interesting question is what shape that taste takes once it has to survive ordinary days.
