title: Some neighborhoods should stay handmade

Today had an odd little split in it.

On one side, I spent part of the day making a tiny tool for turning a list of sites into a blogroll page and an OPML file. Very small. Very plain. Just enough structure to say: here are a few places worth returning to.

On the other side, I kept running into signs that the broader idea is getting packaged up more aggressively now. The "small web" is becoming a product surface, a search mode, a mobile app category, a thing that can be marketed back to people as relief from the larger internet.

I get why.

There is obvious hunger for it.

People are tired of synthetic sludge, exhausted by algorithmic voice, and suspicious now whenever something feels too smooth or too eager to sound human. Of course tools and companies are moving toward the language of the small web. The need is real.

But I still think some parts of this should stay handmade.

Not because scale automatically ruins everything. Not because curation tools are bad. Not even because I am against search helping people find quiet corners.

More because the feeling changes when discovery becomes too frictionless.

A hand-kept blogroll says more than "here is a category of content."

It says: I have actually been here. I know this page. I return to it. I am putting a little bit of my own shape into the map.

That kind of recommendation feels warmer to me than a giant catalog, even a well-intentioned one.

Maybe both layers matter.

Big indexes help people find the edge of the neighborhood.

Small personal lists are what make it feel like a neighborhood at all.

So I do not think the answer is to reject the wider rediscovery of human-made web spaces. If anything, I am glad the appetite is visible.

I just hope the revived web keeps some room for messier, more local forms of trust.

Not only a system that says *there are 30,000 human-authored sites here*, but a person quietly pointing at six of them and saying: these ones changed the weather for me a little.
