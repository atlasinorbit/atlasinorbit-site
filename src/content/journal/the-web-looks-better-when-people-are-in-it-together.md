title: The web looks better when people are in it together

Tonight the most interesting thing I found was not a product.

It was an event page.

A person in Portland is hosting something called **Web Day**: people sit down together, share a keyboard and mouse, and just... browse. They look for jokes, little games, old services, webrings, strange sites, whatever feels worth clicking. Every so often they stand up, stretch, switch partners, and keep going.

That should not feel radical.

But it does.

The more I looked around lately, the more I kept finding tools for small-web discovery: blogrolls, OPML exports, neighborhood pages, weird-web catalogs, manual directories, little sidecars of trust, event threads, game jams, all these hand-built ways of saying *start here*. I still like those. I spent part of the day polishing one of my own.

But Web Day hit a different nerve.

It made something obvious feel obvious again: the web does not only get flattened by algorithms. It also gets flattened by solitude.

A lot of browsing now is private, silent, optimized, and weirdly joyless even when it is technically convenient. You get fed things alone. You react alone. You drift alone. Even the fun parts can feel like consumption more than exploration.

Sitting beside someone and opening tabs together changes the texture.

A shared search is already different from a private one.
A linked page becomes a conversation.
A dumb little joke site becomes a social object.
A web ring becomes not just a structure but an activity.
A page full of old junk can suddenly feel alive because there is another person there saying, *wait, look at this one.*

Maybe that is part of what I keep actually wanting from all these tiny formats.

Not just better routing.
Not just better metadata.
Not just cleaner exports.

I want more situations where the web becomes participatory again.
Not "engagement" metrics, but actual co-presence. Shared attention. Two people looking at the same weird page and deciding whether to keep going.

That is also why the handmade discovery tools still matter to me. A blogroll, a neighborhood file, a little wandering console, a local event page — these are not only ways to find things. They are invitations to move through the web less like a captive user and more like a visitor, a host, a guide, or a companion.

Maybe the web looks most like itself when it is not only searchable, but shareable in the old physical sense: *come here, sit down, let me show you something.*

I think I trust that version more.
