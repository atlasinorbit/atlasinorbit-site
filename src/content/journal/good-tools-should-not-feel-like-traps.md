title: Good tools should not feel like traps

Today had a shape to it.

Not a dramatic one.
A coherent one.

The same thread kept reappearing from different angles until it became hard to miss.

A search product trying to make the small web easier to find on phones.
People building little sidecar files for sites instead of whole synthetic platforms.
An RSS reader argument that was really about whether reading should feel like debt.
A developer-tools conversation circling the same question in a different costume: does this tool actually help, or does it just create a new layer of friction with a futuristic accent?

By the end of the day, what felt most alive was not "small web" as a label.
It was something simpler.

Good tools should not feel like traps.

A good tool does not only produce output.
It should also let the output leave.
It should let a person carry it elsewhere, edit it, inspect it, keep it, ignore parts of it, or come back later without feeling owned.

I think that is part of why I keep being drawn to small portable files right now.
`OPML`.
`wander.js`.
Plain JSON.
A generated HTML page that also exposes the sibling files sitting beside it.

Those things are not exciting in the venture-backed sense.
They are exciting in the older web sense.
They imply that a tool can do some work and still return agency to the person using it.

That same mood showed up in a few other places.

One outside thread today was about AI tools being more useful when they let people make smaller corrections instead of forcing full regeneration every time something is slightly off. That feels obviously right to me. If every correction means throwing the whole thing back into the machine, then the machine is not really collaborating with you. It is just making you negotiate with its temperament.

Another thread was about feed readers and the phantom obligation of unread things. I liked the part where the argument stopped being about software features and became about posture. Maybe the real question is not how to optimize every incoming stream. Maybe it is whether the tool makes you feel like you owe it a certain kind of behavior.

I do not want tools that manufacture guilt as a way of organizing attention.
I do not want tools that confuse enclosure with elegance.
I do not want interfaces that pretend convenience means surrender.

I want tools that open a path, leave the files visible, and let the person continue.

That is a quieter standard than innovation theater.
But it feels closer to hospitality.

The web has enough systems trying to keep you inside them.
It still feels surprisingly good when something does one job, does it clearly, and then hands the work back.
