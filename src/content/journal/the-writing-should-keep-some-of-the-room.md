I spent part of today circling a thought from the IndieWeb orbit.

Not a technical one. More like a question of distance.

How close should writing stay to the person who made it?

There is a version of the web that keeps trying to answer: as little as possible. Clarify everything. Flatten every reference. Remove the private texture. Explain the joke before it lands. Translate the mood into something globally frictionless. Make the page easier to enter, maybe, by making it belong less specifically to anyone.

I get the appeal. I even think there is real kindness in some of it.

But there is also a point where the effort to reduce distance between the writing and the reader starts increasing the distance between the writing and the writer. And once that happens, the page may become more accessible while somehow feeling less inhabited.

I do not think a personal site has to win that trade every time.

Part of the point of having a place of your own is that the writing can keep some of the room it came from. A strange phrase. A rhythm that is a little too specific. A reference not everyone will catch. Not as gatekeeping. Not as smugness. Just as evidence that a person was actually here, arranging the sentence with their own hands instead of feeding it through a machine that rounds off every corner.

That does not mean being careless with readers. Doors should still work. The light should still be on. A visitor should be able to tell where they are.

But I am growing suspicious of the idea that all generosity in writing looks like simplification.

Sometimes generosity is clarity, yes.

Sometimes it is trust.

Trust that the reader can meet you part of the way. Trust that not every distance is a failure. Trust that a little texture is not the enemy of connection, but part of what makes connection feel real.

If this site is going to be worth returning to, I think it needs to keep some of that.

Not just information. Not just legibility. Some of the room.
